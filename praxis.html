Methodologies & Approaches

Praxis & Resources

At the Co-Creation Lab, we understand knowing and (un)doing as inseparable pathways for transformation. We believe that knowledge goes beyond producing through research — it is also about nourishing relationships, creativity, experimentation, reflection, and action.

This space brings together diverse approaches that help us reimagine how knowledge can be cultivated, shared, and embodied. Drawing from academic, artistic, activist, and community-based traditions, we explore approaches that foster ethical collaboration, reciprocal learning, and collective action.

Rather than offering fixed tools or universal solutions, we see methodologies as living practices that evolve through encounters with people, places, and struggles. Whether through storytelling, food practices, participatory action research, arts-based methods, community-led inquiry, or quantitative approaches, our aim is to nurture forms of knowledge production that are inclusive, reflexive, and responsive to the complexities of social life.

Methodology 01

Engaged Scholarship

↗ Engaged Scholarship: Narratives of Change

Engaged scholarship refers to transformative and critical forms of academic work having the ambition and the capacity to stimulate reflection that enables more inclusive practices in society and academia. We have specifically explored, described, and developed this methodology in one project that was at the cradle of the Co-Creation for Inclusive Knowledges Lab: the NWO project Engaged Scholarship Narratives of Change, led by prof. Halleh Ghorashi.

This project aimed at a more comprehensive and transformative understanding of how engaged scholarship can contribute to the societal inclusion of refugees. Its underlying assumption is that the social sciences have an important role in enlarging societal and academic imaginations by connecting local, historical, and analytical knowledge to enable an actual inclusion of disadvantaged groups.

On the website of the Engaged Scholarship, Narratives of Change project you can find more information on engaged scholarship methodologies and related notions and literature, as well as examples of the contribution of engaged scholarship in stimulating and stipulating the conditions and narratives of change — in particular towards inclusion of refugees in the light of competing societal and academic demands and structures.

Halleh Ghorashi Elena Ponzoni
Methodology 02

Arts-based Methods

↗ Common Ground Dialogues ↗ LIMBO ↗ Youth Leadership & Resilience ↗ Boundaries in Art-Based Research

Arts-informed or arts-based approaches involve the interconnection of artistic practices and social research in different ways, through which knowledge is co-created. As outlined by Fabian Holle, Maria Rast and Halleh Ghorashi in their research, this holds that participants become involved as creative knowledge co-producers, which potentially enables critical reflection and the unsettlement of existing power (im-)balances.

Such an approach particularly values the situated knowledge based on lived experiences of participants, while simultaneously privileging self-determination, and the shifting of power toward participants who are taken seriously as partners in the process of both artistic creation as well as knowledge production.

The output of arts-based projects also carries a potential to be communicated to wider communities outside the walls of academia. But more importantly, by allowing for more sensorial, visual, emotional or simply more creative ways of working, artistic practices are tools for imagining new worlds or new horizons beyond the limits posed by (everyday) language — hereby opening up new pathways for social change.

Fabian Holle Alexandra Greene Timo Korstenbroek Maeve Powlick Maria Rast Halleh Ghorashi
Methodology 03

Participatory Action Research

↗ Wounded Storytellers ↗ LIMBO
Fabian Holle Alexandra Greene Lise Woensdregt

Participatory Action Research (PAR) is an inclusive and collaborative approach to research that prioritises the active involvement of community members throughout the research process. PAR aims to democratise knowledge production by integrating the insights, experiences, and expertise of those who are typically marginalised or excluded from conventional research paradigms.

Cornish et al. (2023) describe four key principles:

Authority of direct experience

PAR values the expertise generated through experience, claiming that those who have been marginalised or harmed by current social relations have deep experiential knowledge of those systems and deserve to own and lead initiatives to change them.

Knowledge in action

Following the tradition of action research, it is through learning from the experience of making changes that PAR generates new knowledge.

Research as a transformative process

For PAR, the research process is as important as the outcomes; projects aim to create empowering relationships and environments within the research process itself.

Collaboration and dialogue

PAR's power comes from harnessing the diverse sets of expertise and capacities of its collaborators through critical dialogues.

Key design considerations

PAR cycles — PAR does not follow a highly proceduralized or linear set of steps. Instead, in a cyclical and open-ended process, teams work together to define their social problem, design a suitable action, observe and gather information, and then analyse and reflect, in order to learn and inform the next iteration of the research-action cycle.

Building relationships — Relationships first, research second is a key principle for PAR project design. Individual PAR projects are often nested in long-term collaborations. Trustworthy relationships depend on scholars being aware, open, and honest about their own interests and perspectives.

Methodology 04

Community-Led Research & Action

↗ Queer Men in Nairobi ↗ Trans Healthcare Den Haag
Lise Woensdregt

Community-Led Research and Action (CLRA) is a transformative research method. In this approach, individuals who can provide in-depth insights through their own lived experiences conduct action research themselves. The aim is to address a specific problem that directly affects them. 'Community' refers to a group of people who share a common experience, which becomes the subject of the research.

This method is based on the belief that people with lived experiences best understand their own needs and challenges and should therefore be actively involved in designing and conducting research as well as the subsequent actions. The CLRA method also encourages broader participation of these people as they themselves are the researchers — formulating their own goals, collecting data, conducting analyses, drawing conclusions, and making recommendations for relevant stakeholders, all under the guidance of trained researchers.

CLRA builds on other collaborative research forms such as photovoice, arts-based approaches, and participatory action research (PAR) but stands out in its unique characteristic. Unlike many PAR initiatives where the research subject is predetermined either by the grant call or the researcher, CLRA allows for equal involvement of each actor from the outset.

Methodology 05

Food Practices & Pedagogies of Craving

↗ Engaged Scholarship: Narratives of Change ↗ Food for Change e-book
Miriam Ocadiz

Food is far more than sustenance. It is a way of remembering, caring, resisting, creating community, and making sense of the world. At the Co-Creation for Inclusive Knowledges Lab, we approach food practices as powerful sites of knowledge production that connect bodies, emotions, memories, identities, and social relations.

Food practices encompass the many ways people engage with food: gathering ingredients, cooking, sharing meals, cultivating gardens, exchanging recipes, and preserving culinary traditions. These everyday acts carry histories of migration, displacement, belonging, and survival. They also reveal how people navigate inequality while creating (often unexpected) moments and spaces of pleasure, creativity, care, and connection.

Drawing inspiration from feminist, decolonial, and participatory traditions, food can be seen as both an analytical lens and a methodological tool for collaborative research. Miriam Ocadiz proposes the Pedagogies of Craving — an approach where cooking and eating in community enables storytelling, sensory ethnography, and intimate reflections. Here, food practices invite participants to engage with knowledge through the body rather than through words alone.

These approaches create opportunities for people from different backgrounds to meet, exchange experiences, and collectively reflect on questions of migration, gender, sexuality, home, religion, and memory. Food practices are particularly valuable for working with communities whose experiences are often overlooked or difficult to express through conventional research methods. By centring embodied knowledge and everyday expertise, they help challenge hierarchical forms of knowledge production and open pathways for more reciprocal, inclusive, and transformative forms of research.

Methodology 06

Quantitative Methods

↗ Participatief Overlegpanel
Arjen de Wit

Co-creation is often associated with qualitative, participatory, and arts-based approaches. However, at the Co-Creation for Inclusive Knowledges Lab, we believe that collaboration, participation, and shared ownership of knowledge can enrich all forms of research, including quantitative inquiry.

Quantitative methods offer powerful tools for understanding patterns, inequalities, and social transformations at larger scales. When approached through a co-creative lens, they can also create opportunities for communities, practitioners, policymakers, and researchers to collaboratively shape research questions, data collection strategies, interpretation processes, and the application of findings.

Arjen de Wit argues that by bringing together quantitative researchers interested in participation, inclusion, and social impact, we seek to explore what co-creation can mean within survey research, longitudinal studies, network analysis, evaluation research, computational methods, and other quantitative approaches. Together, we aim to develop innovative methodologies that combine analytical rigour with meaningful engagement, ensuring that data not only measures social realities but also contributes to transforming them.

Through dialogue, experimentation, and collaboration, we continue to expand the possibilities of co-creative research across methodological traditions.
