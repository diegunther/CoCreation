About the Programme

What is the Spring School?

At a time when questions of inequality, exclusion, and social justice demand new ways of working together, the Spring School invites participants from diverse contexts and backgrounds to facilitate critical reflections on the relationship between knowledge and power. We challenge conventional divides between theory and practice by engaging with methodologies that value lived experience, creativity, and collective action as essential sources of knowledge.

Each year, the Spring School brings together critical scholars, artists, activists, practitioners, and community leaders from different parts of the world. Through workshops, dialogues, creative exercises, and collaborative learning, participants gain resourceful experience with approaches such as arts-based research, storytelling, participatory action research, community-led inquiry, photo fabulation, food practices, and other co-creative methodologies.

More than a training programme, the Spring School is an opportunity to become part of a growing international community committed to cultivating more inclusive, relational, and transformative ways of knowing and doing.

The Spring School welcomes anyone interested in building meaningful collaborations across different knowledge systems, including researchers, students, artists, activists, policymakers, and community practitioners.

We offer affordable fees, tailored to different financial circumstances, and pursue a safe(r) and sensible environment for diverse needs, concerns and languages to make the program more accessible and enjoyable, especially for people historically excluded from academic spaces.

The programme unfolds over four immersive days of dialogue, creativity, reflection, and collective learning.

Co-curated by Elena Ponzoni, Miriam Ocadiz, Sharmila Angoelal, and Timo Korstenbroek

Objectives

  • Critically explore the possibilities and limitations of co-creation
  • Engage with innovative and creative methodologies
  • Learn from practitioners, artists, activists, and scholars from diverse contexts
  • Reflect on questions of power, ethics, and collaboration
  • Build meaningful connections with participants across disciplines and sectors
  • Join a growing network committed to social and environmental justice
First Edition

2025 Spring School

From the 3 to the 6 June 2025 we hosted our first Spring School at the Vrije Universiteit Amsterdam. In this edition, we set the foundation on what is (not) co-creation, exploring the ethics, politics, and practical dimensions of building meaningful collaborations. Guided by 10 facilitators with diverse backgrounds and expertise, we welcomed over 30 participants to reflect on questions of power, inclusion, storytelling, collective knowledge-making, and social transformation through interactive workshops, critical conversations, and creative activities.

A central feature of the programme was the emphasis on learning from lived experience and community-based initiatives. Through poetry, visual storytelling, archival practices, zine-making, and collaborative reflection, participants were invited to critically examine conventional approaches to research and imagine alternative ways of generating knowledge together.

Our Programme Included

Paulina Trejo Méndez
Opening Lecture
+

Paulina shared insights from their experiences co-creating with Indigenous communities in Mexico and Latin American migrant communities in Europe. Their contribution opened up a critical discussion on what co-creation is — and is not — as a pathway to challenge systems of oppression and violence.

Alexandra Greene
Zine Workshop
+

Alexandra guided participants in translating the day's conversations — and their own lived experiences and ideas around co-creation — into a zine. Zines (independently published magazines) have long served as creative and radical mediums through which marginalised and alternative communities share stories, communicate ideas, and build collective knowledge. In this workshop, participants learned about the history and power of zine-making, explored how zines can be used to translate academic and community-based research, and began crafting their own zines inspired by the preceding critical discussion on co-creation.

Saba Hamzah
Living Room: Coming Towards Home, Poetry Workshop
+

Saba facilitated a creative intervention that wove together written and visual poetry to cultivate stories of resistance. This session intersected art and activism, linking participants' personal narratives with ongoing struggles against colonialism, gender inequality, racism, and forced displacement. Saba also explored the importance of co-curating and co-preserving stories that have historically been silenced.

Rochita Ruiz
Worldmaking Workshop
+

Rochita guided us through a thought-provoking exercise in worldmaking as an act of co-creation. Drawing on her expertise as a science fiction writer, participants worked collaboratively to design, imagine, and experiment with alternative worlds where justice, creativity, and care were the norm. Through drawing, dialogue, and collective imagination, the workshop brought diverse visions of possible futures into conversation with one another.

Salma Fayad & Marie-José van Schaik
Community Co-Creation through Community-Based Practices
+

Salma and Marie-José guided a reflective, hands-on session that invited participants to step beyond academic boundaries and root co-creation in lived experience. Together, we explored how co-creation is initiated, sustained, and nurtured through ethics, curiosity, and care. The workshop reflected on group dynamics and demonstrated how community-based practices can strengthen advocacy efforts for social justice.

The Black Archives
Art & Archive Workshop
+

The Black Archives shared insights into their experience of co-creating archives for social causes at the intersection of art, activism, and scholarship. The workshop explored the methodologies behind initiatives such as No Healing Without Reparations and Listening Session: Sudanese Resistance Music. Together, participants reflected on how archives can function as living spaces for dialogue, memory-making, and co-creation, while becoming powerful tools for justice and social transformation.

Halleh Ghorashi & Busisiwe Ntsele
Conversation and Reflection
+

Busisiwe (Busi) Ntsele shared her personal journey as a co-creative PhD scholar. This session opened a conversation about conducting engaged research within academia, navigating institutional constraints, and sustaining co-creative practices from the margins.

Halleh Ghorashi led an open dialogue on engaged scholarship and the politics of co-creation in increasingly pressured academic environments. Together with Halleh and Busi, participants explored how to cultivate spaces of resistance, solidarity, and meaningful knowledge exchange within the university.

Arjen de Wit
Workshop on Co-Creation in Quantitative Research
+

Arjen facilitated a discussion aimed at unpacking and expanding our understanding of quantitative research, challenging conventional assumptions about what constitutes valid knowledge. By connecting storytelling, lived experiences, and academic inquiry, this session explored both the tensions and possibilities of transforming quantitative research into a space for critical co-creation.

Thomars Shamuyarira & Miriam Ocadiz
Pedagogies of Craving
+

This closing workshop invited participants to explore and embody desire, imagination, and longing as tools within co-creative practices. Thomars and Mimi shared their experiences of co-creation in South Africa and introduced the notion of pedagogies of craving — a call to acknowledge and honour our appetites for nourishment, connectivity, and justice. Together, participants reflected on what it means to learn from individual and collective cravings for connection and change, both within and beyond the academy, through the visceral acts of communal cooking and eating.

Second Edition

Spring School 2026

This second edition took place from the 19 to the 22 of May 2026. This time we explored further pathways of co-creation, collaborating with Dutch-based communities, as well as bridging dialogues with queer collectives, visual storytelling, and international artivists.

Across four days, participants engaged with community-led initiatives, queer and decolonial approaches, arts-based methodologies, storytelling practices, and creative forms of inquiry. Together, we explored how co-creation can challenge dominant hierarchies of knowledge, blur the boundaries between theory and practice, and create new possibilities for addressing social and environmental injustices.

The programme intentionally moved between university spaces and community-led organisations, creating opportunities to learn from grassroots initiatives that are already cultivating alternative ways of organising, imagining, and acting. Memory and fabulation took a central role through photo-performance, storytelling, and zine-making, highlighting the power of queer communities to expand co-creative artivism. Participants were encouraged not only to discuss co-creation, but to actively experience and practice it.

Our Programme Included

Halleh Ghorashi
Welcome Lecture: Engaged Scholarship and Co-Creation
+

Halleh Ghorashi reflected on her journey as a pioneer of engaged scholarship, drawing from her experiences as a forced migrant, scholar, and public intellectual. Through stories of research, policymaking, and community engagement, she explored the possibilities and challenges of co-creating knowledge within institutions that often reproduce inequalities.

GROND & Afrikaanderwijk Cooperative
Community Learning Day
+

Participants spent a day outside the university engaging with community-led initiatives committed to collectivity, sustainability, and social justice. Through a guided exploration of GROND and a dialogue with representatives from Afrikaanderwijk Cooperative, participants reflected on power, representation, solidarity, and the possibilities of supporting community-led agendas through co-creative practices.

Afrontosas
Fabulation and Photo-Performance
+

Drawing inspiration from their project Where Are They?, the Afro-diasporic queer collective Afrontosas guided participants through an artistic exploration of memory, visibility, and resistance. Using photo-performance, costume, embodiment, and instant photography, participants collectively created alternative archives and acts of defiant memory from queer and dissident perspectives.

LIMBO
Queering Co-Creation
+

Members of LIMBO, a queer refugee and migrant collective in the Netherlands, facilitated a creative workshop exploring storytelling, performativity, and speculative imagination. Building on the concept of fabulation, participants reflected on how personal and collective histories can be reimagined through artistic and community-led practices.

Famo Musa
Visual Storytelling Workshop
+

Artist, poet, and community organizer Famo Musa invited participants to engage with storytelling through photography, creative writing, and oral narratives. Drawing on her extensive work with migrant and refugee communities, she explored how stories can be embodied, shared, and preserved across languages and generations.

Alexandra Greene
Zine-Making Workshop
+

Alexandra Greene guided participants in transforming their experiences, reflections, and emotions from the Spring School into collaborative zines. Through cutting, writing, drawing, and assembling, participants experimented with zines as accessible tools for storytelling, research dissemination, activism, and collective knowledge-making. This workshop was a continuation of the 2025 initiative to develop a zine for the Spring School across the years.

Zineb Gormat
A Sense of Home: Migration and Storytelling
+

Drawing on her research with sub-Saharan African migrant children in Morocco, Zineb Gormat facilitated a workshop on belonging, resilience, and agency. Through storytelling, dialogue, and performative exercises, participants reflected on their own experiences of belonging and un-belonging while engaging with migration as a deeply relational and human experience.
